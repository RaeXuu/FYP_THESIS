**CHAPTER 1**

**INTRODUCTION**

1.1 Research Background and Motivation

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is the leading cause of global mortality,
responsible for approximately 17.9 million deaths each year according to
the World Health Organization. Early screening and continuous monitoring
of cardiac function are critical for the prevention and timely
intervention of CVD. Heart sound auscultation, as a non-invasive,
low-cost primary diagnostic method, has been a cornerstone of cardiac
clinical practice for over 200 years. Traditional acoustic stethoscopes,
however, have significant limitations: they rely entirely on the
subjective experience of clinicians, provide no quantitative data
recording or retrospective analysis, and are unable to support remote or
home-based long-term monitoring.

With the development of embedded systems and wireless communication
technology, digital stethoscopes have emerged as a promising solution to
address these limitations. Commercial digital stethoscopes such as the
Eko Core have demonstrated the clinical value of digitized heart sound
acquisition, but their high cost (over \$300 per unit) and complex
operation limit their popularity in resource-limited settings and
household scenarios. This motivates the development of a low-cost,
portable, easy-to-use digital heart sound stethoscope, which can realize
real-time acquisition,wireless transmission, and quantitative analysis
of heart sound signals, making cardiac health monitoring accessible to
the general public.

1.2 Problem Statement and Research Objectives

Existing academic and commercial digital stethoscope solutions have
three key limitations that this project aims to address:

(1) High cost and complex hardware design: Most existing solutions use
    multi-chip architectures with separate audio codec, microcontroller,
    and Bluetooth modules, increasing the bill of materials (BOM) cost
    and hardware complexity.

(2) Poor real-time performance of wireless transmission: Many solutions
    use traditional polling-based Bluetooth transmission, which
    introduces high latency and data loss for continuous audio
    streaming.

(3) Lack of integrated signal processing and analysis tools: Most
    low-cost solutions only provide raw data acquisition, without
    integrated filtering, feature extraction, and visual analysis
    functions for heart sound signals.

Based on the above background, this project focuses on the specific
application scenario of \"Digital Bluetooth stethoscope\", and mainly
studies and solves the following key problems:

(1) How to design and implement a low-power, miniaturized digital
    Bluetooth stethoscope hardware system: capable of stably and clearly
    collecting heart sound signals and achieving wireless transmission;

(2) How to effectively preprocess and extract features from the
    collected heart sound signals: improve signal quality and reduce the
    impact of environmental noise and individual differences;

(3) How to build an intelligent diagnostic model suitable for heart
    sound signals: While ensuring diagnostic accuracy, taking into
    account model complexity and computing resource consumption;

(4) How to achieve the collaborative work of algorithms and actual
    systems: Complete the entire closed loop from signal acquisition,
    data transmission, model reasoning to result presentation.

By solving the above problems, a prototype system of digital Bluetooth
stethoscope with practical application value was ultimately realized.

1.3 Research Methodology

This project adopts a modular design methodology, dividing the system
into three independent and interconnected modules: the ESP32-based heart
sound acquisition terminal, BLE wireless transmission link, and
Raspberry Pi-based edge processing and intelligent diagnosis platform.
The hardware design uses a single-chip ESP32-C3 solution to integrate
high-fidelity audio acquisition and BLE wireless data transmission,
reducing hardware complexity and overall cost while ensuring stable
real-time signal acquisition. For firmware development, the Arduino
framework is used for ESP32 development, to implement the I2S audio
driver, BLE data encapsulation, and low-latency active data streaming
via the Notify mode. For the upper-layer processing system, development
is carried out in the Linux environment of the Raspberry Pi, with Python
as the core development language, ensuring cross-environment
compatibility and ease of secondary development. In the field of signal
processing, three-order high-pass and second-order Butterworth band-pass
filters were designed based on the frequency characteristics of the
heart sound signal. These filters were combined with the median
filtering algorithm to achieve heart sound denoising. For intelligent
diagnosis, a lightweight CNN-LSTM deep learning model is built and
deployed on the Raspberry Pi, which is trained on a public clinical
heart sound dataset to realize automatic classification and diagnosis of
normal and abnormal heart sound signals.

2.1 Physiological Basis of Heart Sound Signals

![](media/image1.png){width="6.493055555555555in"
height="1.6680555555555556in"}Heart sounds are acoustic signals
generated by the vibration of the heart valves, myocardium, and blood
flow during the cardiac cycle. A normal heart sound cycle consists of
two main components: the first heart sound (S1) and the second heart
sound (S2), as shown in Fig. 1. S1 is generated by the closure of the
atrioventricular valves at the onset of ventricular systole, with a
frequency range of 20-150Hz. S2 is produced by the closure of the
semilunar valves at the start of ventricular diastole, with a similar
frequency range to S1. The frequency of normal heart sounds is
concentrated between 20Hz and 600Hz, with the most clinically
significant information located in the 20-150Hz band.

Fig. 1 The time-domain waveform of normal heart sounds

Heart sound signals are typically low-amplitude and low-frequency, with
a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) that is easily degraded by environmental
noise, breathing sounds, and electromagnetic interference. Therefore,
effective noise suppression and targeted frequency band extraction are
critical for heart sound signal processing. The sampling rate for heart
sound acquisition must meet the Nyquist sampling theorem; a sampling
rate of 8000Hz is widely used in existing research, as it fully covers
the effective frequency range of heart sounds while minimizing data
volume for wireless transmission.

2.2 Research Status of Digital Stethoscope Technology

2.2.1 Medical Equipment Design

Digital stethoscope technology has developed rapidly over the past two
decades, with research and commercial products focusing on three core
directions: miniaturized hardware design, wireless transmission
optimization, and intelligent signal analysis.

In terms of commercial products, the Eko Core digital stethoscope (Eko
Devices, USA) is the most widely used solution, supporting simultaneous
heart sound and ECG acquisition, 40x sound amplification, and wireless
data transmission to a mobile application,as shown in Fig.2. However,
its high retail price (over \$300) limits its adoption in consumer and
resource-limited clinical settings.

![](media/image2.png){width="4.1in" height="1.4902777777777778in"}

Fig. 2 Eko Smart Stethoscope

![](media/image3.png){width="2.9319444444444445in"
height="1.8284722222222223in"}Apple's Apple Watch Series 4 and later
models have also emerged as popular consumer health monitoring devices
with cardiac health capabilities---they integrate electrode-based ECG
sensors and high-precision heart rate monitors, enabling users to
measure single-lead ECGs, detect atrial fibrillation (AFib), and track
resting/active heart rates in real time,as shown in Fig.3.While the
Apple Watch excels in portability, integration with daily wearables, and
user-friendly mobile connectivity, it is not a dedicated stethoscope: it
lacks specialized heart sound acquisition hardware (e.g.,
high-sensitivity microphones optimized for chest acoustics) and cannot
capture or analyze phonocardiogram (PCG) signals, limiting its utility
for heart sound-based clinical screening.

Fig. 3 Using the Apple Watch for heart monitoring

In academic research, numerous studies have proposed low-cost digital
stethoscope solutions based on microcontrollers. Wang et al. from the
University of Chinese Academy of Sciences developed a portable
electronic stethoscope using the STM32F103RET6 microprocessor, selecting
the OB4015L electret microphone after comparative testing and optimizing
the auscultation head via finite element simulation. However, this
solution relies on a discrete hardware architecture: weak analog heart
sound signals require independent DSP filtering (600Hz low-pass),
TPA6100 amplification (up to 11x), and on-chip ADC conversion before
transmission to the host via external Bluetooth/serial modules. This
design increases hardware complexity, introduces analog signal
loss/interference risks, and relies on complex conditioning circuits for
the electret microphone---limiting miniaturization and
cost-effectiveness.Similar to those also using STM32 as the main
controller, an independent Bluetooth module will be employed. This will
increase the complexity of the hardware and the burden on the power
module.

2.2.2 Relevant Diagnostic Algorithms Evolution

Before the popularization of deep learning, the classification of heart
sounds mainly relied on feature engineering driven by expert knowledge.
The review articles by Li et al. and Ben Hamza et al. detailed the
technical routes of this stage. The mainstream features include Mayer
frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCC), short-time Fourier transform
(STFT) spectrographs, and time-domain statistical features (such as
energy entropy and zero-passing rate). The research of Yaseen et al.
demonstrated a typical workflow: constructing high-dimensional feature
vectors by extracting multiple time-frequency domain features, and
comparing the classification effects of Support Vector Machine (SVM),
K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN), and Random Forest. Research has found that SVM
performs stably on small sample datasets, but traditional methods are
highly dependent on the quality of feature extraction and have weak
generalization ability, making it difficult to cope with the highly
variable pathological signals in the clinical environment.

With the release of large public datasets such as PhysioNet, deep
learning (DL) has gradually taken the lead. Ren et al. and Partovi et
al. reviewed that convolutional neural networks (CNNS) have become the
preferred choice for processing heart sound spectra due to their
powerful spatial feature extraction capabilities. Guo et al. trained a
deep CNN model for pulmonary arterial hypertension screening using a
large-scale dataset containing approximately 6,000 samples,
demonstrating the advantages of deep models in mining deep pathological
features. Considering the time series characteristics of heart sound
signals, Chen et al. pointed out that the hybrid architecture (CRNN)
that combines CNN to extract frequency-domain features with recurrent
neural networks (RNNS, such as LSTM/GRU) to extract temporal
dependencies is a research hotspot during this period. The comparative
study by Ameen et al. also indicates that, compared with
electrocardiogram (ECG) signals, the classification of PCG signals is
more dependent on deep feature learning after time-frequency conversion.

\"Edge AI\" has become a bridge connecting high-precision algorithms
with low-cost hardware currently. Zhang et al. provided a direct
reference example for this topic in their work in 2023. They designed a
low-cost digital stethoscope based on Raspberry PI Zero 2W (with a
hardware cost of only about \$25) and proposed a lightweight hybrid
model of CNN and random forest. The real-time detection of heart and
lung diseases was successfully achieved on the Raspberry PI with limited
computing power, demonstrating the feasibility of the edge computing
architecture. In addition, Jumphoo et al. explored the use of
data-efficient image Transformer (DeiT) for transfer learning. Through
model pruning and quantization techniques, they demonstrated that
advanced models, after optimization, can also be adapted to edge
application scenarios with small samples and low computing power. These
studies show that through the hierarchical architecture of \"ESP32
acquisition + Raspberry PI edge inference\", the system cost and power
consumption can be significantly reduced while ensuring diagnostic
accuracy, promoting the popularization of intelligent auscultation
technology.

2.3 Core Technical Principles

2.3.1 I2S Audio Bus Protocol

The Inter-IC Sound (I2S) bus is a serial synchronous communication
protocol specifically designed for digital audio transmission between
integrated circuits. It uses three signal lines for full-duplex audio
transmission:

(1) Serial Clock (SCK): The bit clock signal, with one clock cycle
    corresponding to one bit of audio data.

(2) Word Select (WS): The frame synchronization signal, which indicates
    the channel of the current audio data (0 for left channel, 1 for
    right channel).

(3) Serial Data (SD): The serial audio data line, transmitting the PCM
    audio samples in two\'s complement format.

![](media/image4.png){width="4.8069444444444445in"
height="2.259027777777778in"}The precise timing relationships between
these signals, confirming the synchronous data transfer and stereo
channel separation, are illustrated in Fig. 4, which details the
Standard Mode.In this project, the ESP32-C3 is configured as the I2S
master, generating the SCK and WS signals to drive the INMP441 MEMS
microphone (I2S slave). This configuration ensures stable timing
synchronization and high-fidelity audio data acquisition, with no need
for additional PDM to PCM conversion required by traditional digital
microphones.

Fig. 4 I2S Bus Timing Diagrams for Standard and Left-Justified Formats

2.3.2 BLE Low Energy Communication Protocol

Bluetooth is a standard for short-range wireless data exchange operating
in the unlicensed 2.4 GHz Industrial, Scientific, and Medical (ISM)
band. Modern Bluetooth devices generally fall into two categories:
Bluetooth Basic Rate/Enhanced Data Rate (BR/EDR), also known as Classic
Bluetooth, and Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE). While BR/EDR is optimized for
continuous data streaming such as high-quality audio, BLE is
specifically designed for low-bandwidth applications with significantly
lower power consumption and costs.

The BLE protocol has evolved through several versions, from BLE 4.2 to
the latest BLE 5.x standards. Starting from BLE 5.0, the protocol
introduced a 2 Mbps PHY layer, which doubles the data rate compared to
previous versions and significantly improves transmission efficiency for
audio-related tasks. In this project, the BLE 5.0 protocol is selected
as it provides a robust balance between energy efficiency and the
throughput required for real-time heart sound streaming.

In this project, the communication architecture is built upon the
Generic Access Profile (GAP) and the Generic Attribute Profile (GATT) to
ensure efficient heart sound data streaming,At the network level,
![](media/image5.png){width="4.084027777777778in"
height="2.3354166666666667in"}GAP defines the interaction roles where
the ESP32 acts as a Peripheral and the receiving host serves as the
Central device. The process begins with the ESP32 broadcasting its
presence, allowing the host to discover and establish a stable
point-to-point link. Once connected, the GATT layer organizes the data
into a hierarchical structure of Services and Characteristics. Each
characteristic is identified by a unique UUID, acting as a dedicated
data pipe for the downsampled signals.The GATT structure diagram is
shown in Fig. 5.

Fig. 5 Generalized Hierarchical Structure of BLE GATT Profile

The core of the streaming efficiency lies in the Notify property.As
shown in Fig.6, Unlike the traditional Read mode---which requires the
host to manually request each packet---the Notify mechanism allows the
ESP32 to push data to the host as soon as it is sampled. This
asynchronous transfer eliminates the latency of request-response
handshakes, making it the ideal choice for maintaining the temporal
integrity of real-time audio. To maintain a high signal-to-noise ratio
without the overhead of complex codecs, the system transmits downsampled
raw data, ensuring the host receives the necessary frequency components
of the heart sound without unnecessary bandwidth consumption.

![](media/image6.png){width="4.084027777777778in"
height="2.2291666666666665in"}

Fig. 6 BLE Data Transfer Models: Read vs. Notify

2.4 Overall Framework of the System

The overall design of this system mainly consists of three parts,
including the data acquisition hardware terminal, the Raspberry Pi
receiving and processing end, and the automatic heart sound diagnosis
algorithm. The overall structure block diagram of the system is shown in
Fig.7.

![](media/image7.png){width="5.065972222222222in"
height="2.6631944444444446in"}

Fig. 7 Overall framework of the system

The hardware system is mainly composed of MEMS microphones, power
management modules and ESP32 central control units. The front end of the
collection uses highly sensitive MEMS microphones to capture heart sound
(PCG) acoustic wave signals and directly encodes them into digital audio
streams. The power module is responsible for providing stable power
support for the entire front-end acquisition system. ESP32 is
responsible for the initial caching and downsampling processing of the
collected signals, and then uses the Notify mode in the BLE 5.0 protocol
to upload the data to the receiving end in real time to ensure low power
consumption and low latency transmission.

The receiving end (Raspberry Pi) is responsible for receiving and
parsing the Bluetooth data. This part first establishes a connection by
calling the Bluetooth module and continuously acquires the heart sound
data stream actively pushed by the transmitting end. Subsequently, the
received data enters the signal processing module, where digital signal
processing algorithms (such as filtering and normalization) are used to
remove environmental noise and extract the effective heart sound feature
signals.

The automatic diagnosis algorithm part utilizes a pre-trained AI deep
learning model to conduct real-time analysis and reasoning on the
processed heart sound data. The algorithm can initially screen and
identify abnormal pathological heart sounds, and store the final results
in the local database for users\' reference.

**CHAPTER 3**

**SYSTEM HARDWARE DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION**

The hardware terminal of the digital stethoscope is designed with the
requirements of miniaturization, low power consumption, and low cost in
mind. The overall hardware architecture consists of four core functional
modules: the power management module, the heart sound acquisition
module, the main control and wireless transmission module, and the

3.1 Design Evolution of the Hardware System

The core logic of the hardware design is formed through iterative
optimization based on practical development bottlenecks, rather than a
one-time finalized solution. This section first elaborates the initial
design scheme, analyzes the critical performance bottlenecks encountered
in the development process, and lays a logical foundation for the final
hardware component selection and circuit design.

3.1.1 Initial Scheme : nRF52832 + LMD2718T

The initial hardware scheme was built around the nRF52832 BLE
microcontroller and the LMD2718T MEMS digital microphone, with the core
design intent to achieve ultra-low power consumption, compact size, and
portability for the digital stethoscope, its block diagram is shown in
Fig.8.

The specific design considerations of the scheme are as follows:

(1) The nRF52832, a mainstream BLE SoC from Nordic Semiconductor, was

3.1.2 Critical Bottlenecks and Challenges of the Initial Scheme

In the early development and prototype testing phase, the scheme

requirements of the heart sound acquisition system:

(1) Protocol and Interface Conflict

(2) Transmission Bandwidth Limitation

The primary limitation resided in the UART interface of the nRF52832.
While sufficient for low-speed command transmission, the UART bandwidth
proved inadequate for the continuous, high-throughput requirements of
real-time audio streaming. The theoretical maximum baud rate of UART
(115200 bps) cannot meet the bandwidth requirement of continuous 8kHz

data congestion and an inability to maintain an isochronous data flow to
the host device.

(3) Real-time Stability and Data Integrity Risks

The intense CPU demand for software PDM decoding created a resource
conflict with the BLE protocol stack, which requires deterministic
timing to maintain connections. This conflict resulted in an increased
risk of buffer overflows and data loss during the acquisition process.
the steep learning curve of the nRF SDK and the necessity of writing
low-level drivers from scratch posed an unpredictable development
timeline. Given the project\'s strict delivery schedule, the lack of a
stable, verifiable real-time transmission pipeline made the
nRF52832-based scheme engineering unfeasible.

The above bottlenecks made the scheme completely unfeasible in practical
application, forcing a comprehensive re-evaluation and re-selection of
the core hardware components of the system, starting with the
microcontroller and the acoustic sensor.

4.3 Implementation and Comparison of Signal Preprocessing Algorithms

4.3.1 Overview of Pretreatment Objectives and Processes

Due to the extremely weak signal of heart sounds , it is inevitably
affected by respiratory baseline drift, environmental high-frequency
noise and electromagnetic interference during the acquisition process.
In order to extract high-purity S1 and S2 features for subsequent
recognition by the CNN model, this system designs an adaptive
preprocessing pipeline on the Python side. This process mainly includes:
median filtering denoising, baseline drift suppression, and the
selection of filtering algorithms for the fidelity of deep features.

4.3.2 Pulse Interference Elimination and Primary Smoothing

During the process of heart sound collection, due to the influence of
poor instantaneous contact between the sensor and the body surface,
human body movement jitter, and occasional error codes in serial port
transmission, the original signal will show irregular instantaneous
pulse spikes and burrs. Such non-stationary noise will seriously
interfere with the boundary recognition of heart sound cycles and even
lead to deviations in subsequent heart rate calculations.

This system selects nonlinear median filtering as the core scheme for
eliminating impulse noise.Median filtering is a nonlinear sorted
statistical filtering proposed by Tukey in 1971. Its core idea is to
replace the original sampling value at the center of the window with the
median of all sampling values within the sliding window, thereby
eliminating abnormal pulse points that deviate greatly from the
surrounding sampling values while retaining the edge features of the
signal. Its mathematical expression is：

y\[n\]=median{x\[n−k\],...,x\[n\],...,x\[n+k\]}

In the formula:

x\[n\] represents the nth sampling point of the original heart sound
signal as input;

y\[n\] represents the nth sample point of the filtered output.

k represents the half-length of the filtering kernel, and the total
length of the filtering window is 2k+1.

median{·} refers to the operation of calculating the median, which means
that all the sampled values within the window are sorted by amplitude,
and the value at the middle position is taken as the output.

The sampling rate of the heart sound signals in this system is 8000Hz,
meaning the time interval between each sample is 0.125ms. The duration
of the first heart sound (S1) in a normal heart sound is typically
100-200ms, and the duration of the steep rising edge of this sound is
usually less than 5ms. Based on the time-domain characteristics of the
heart sound signals, this system sets the half-length of the filtering
kernel as k=2, corresponding to a total filtering window length of 5
samples (i.e., the kernel size is 5), and the total time length of the
window is

5 × 0.125ms = 0.625ms

The core basis for parameter selection is as follows:

(1) Minimum window length: If a smaller kernel is selected (with a
    window length of 3 sampling points, k = 1), only isolated spikes at
    a single sampling point can be eliminated. The ability to suppress
    continuous burrs and wide pulse noise involving multiple sampling
    points is insufficient. It cannot completely cover the continuous
    pulse interference caused by clothing friction and probe
    displacement.

(2) Window length limit: If a larger kernel is selected (with a window
    length of be equal or greater than 7 sampling points, k≥3), the
    total window time length exceeds 0.875ms, which will have a
    smoothing effect on the steep rising/descending edges of the S1
    heart sound, blurring the abrupt change edges of the heart sound,
    causing deviations in the position of subsequent peak detection and
    directly affecting the accuracy of heart rate calculation;

(3) Optimal Parameter Matching: The filter window with a kernel size
    of 5. While completely eliminating the pulse spikes of
    single/multiple sampling points, the window duration is much shorter
    than the characteristic duration of the S1 heart sound. It can 100%
    retain the time-domain abrupt edges of the heart sound signal,
    without any feature blurring or distortion. It perfectly matches the
    heart sound acquisition scenario of this system with an 8kHz
    sampling rate.

4.3.3 Baseline Drift Suppression (BDR Optimization)

To address the issue of low-frequency Baseline Drift caused by breathing
movements and slow sensor displacement, the system designs a third-order
Butterworth high-pass filter with a cut-off frequency set at 4Hz.
Combining the FPC circuit board design with shielding film adopted at
the hardware end and the optimization of the microphone sealing and
sound insulation structure, this solution can effectively suppress the
ultra-low frequency baseline fluctuations caused by breathing, keep the
baseline drift rate at a relatively low level, and at the same time
avoid the false filtering of the effective low-frequency components of
heart sounds, providing a baseline-stable input signal for subsequent
bandpass filtering.

4.3.4 Comparison of filter algorithm selection: Cascaded IIR filtering
vs. wavelet denoising

To find the most suitable denoising scheme for the portable edge
computing scenario of this system, this study compared two mainstream
biomedical signal denoising algorithms in the effective frequency band
of the heart sound core:

(1) Cascading IIR filtering scheme: It adopts a 4-order Butterworth
    bandpass filter, with the passband locked to the effective frequency
    band of the heart sound core ranging from 20 to 220Hz. Zero-phase
    filtering (FILT-FILT) is used to achieve signal processing without
    phase distortion.

(2) Wavelet denoising scheme: By using db6 wavelet for 6-layer
    decomposition and combining with the soft threshold function to
    perform threshold contraction on the high-frequency noise
    components, the pure heart sound signal is reconstructed.

By collecting the actual heart sounds of a human body, the waveforms

number in square brackets. For example, "More details on the instruction
formats of the processor can be found in the Processor Instruction
Manual \[7\] ...".

Each listed reference should contain complete information for the reader
to easily search for it, should there be a need for this. A consistent
format should be adopted for each type of reference in the reference
list. Examples of (1) a journal reference, (2) an article or chapter in
a book compilation or conference proceedings, (3) a book reference, and
(4) a thesis reference are shown below.

+----------------------------------------------------------------+
| > **REFERENCES**                                               |
|                                                                |
| \[1\] Author(s) name(s), "Article Title", *Journal Title*      |
| **volume \#**, starting page-ending page (year of              |
| publication).                                                  |
|                                                                |
| \[2\] Author(s) name(s), "Article Title" *in* "Book Title,"    |
| name of editor, ed., Publisher, City, starting page-ending     |
| page if applicable (year of publication).                      |
|                                                                |
| \[3\] Author(s) name(s), "Book Title", Publisher, City,        |
| starting page-ending page if applicable (year of publication). |
|                                                                |
| \[4\] Author's name, "Thesis Title", name of university (year  |
| of publication).                                               |
|                                                                |
| Page Number                                                    |
|                                                                |
| *« Page No »*                                                  |
